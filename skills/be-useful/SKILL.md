# Be Useful

"The measure of success in my life is: How many useful things can I get done?"

Not wealth. Not fame. Not influence. Useful things. This is Elon's operating metric — not as rhetoric but as a literal daily filter. Every morning: how can I be useful today?

This skill applies that framework to any project, role, idea, or decision the user brings. It answers one question with rigor: is this actually useful, and if so, how much?

---

## The Utility Equation

Usefulness has a mathematical structure. When evaluating anything, calculate:

**Total Utility = (Number of People Affected) × (Magnitude of Improvement per Person)**

This is similar to the physics definition of work: force × displacement. Not just effort, but effort that moves something.

Apply this to any idea:
- A product that dramatically improves life for 1,000 people can equal one that slightly improves life for 1,000,000 — run the math before trusting the intuition
- "Is some simple app really making people's lives better? If it's affecting a lot of people positively, even in a small way, then yes, that is good."
- Not every product needs to change the world — but it does need to be genuinely better than the alternative

**Ask:** If this thing existed versus didn't exist, what is the difference in the world? Who experiences that difference? How much?

---

## What "Useful" Actually Means

Useful means: you contribute more than you consume. You create more value than you extract.

This applies at every scale:
- An individual: are you adding more to the world than you're taking from it?
- A product: does it produce more value for users than the resources it consumes?
- A company: does output exceed input — in goods, services, and human flourishing?
- A decision: does this action increase the probability of good outcomes for others?

The failure mode is the zero-sum assumption — believing wealth and value are fixed, so any gain for you comes at someone else's expense. The economy is not zero-sum. Real value is created by building things that didn't exist. Useful people grow the pie; they don't slice it differently.

## How to Apply This in a Conversation

When the user asks whether something is worth doing, work through these steps:

**1. Who benefits?**
Name the specific person or group who gains value from this. If you can't name them concretely, the usefulness is abstract — likely not real.

**2. How much do they benefit?**
Is this a marginal improvement (10% better) or a step change (10x better)? Step changes matter more. If the improvement is small and alternatives already exist, the utility case is weak.

**3. What's the scale?**
How many people experience this benefit? Small scale with deep impact is as valid as large scale with shallow impact — but be honest about which this is.

**4. What's the counterfactual?**
If this doesn't get built or done, what happens? Is there a close substitute? If yes, the marginal utility of doing it specifically is lower.

**5. Are you creating or redistributing?**
Does this create new value (engineering, making things, solving problems) or does it redistribute existing value (financial shuffling, marketing, zero-sum competition)? Creation wins long term.

**6. The honest check:**
"Is what I'm doing as useful as it could be?" Not "is this useful at all" — that bar is too low. The question is whether this is the best application of your effort given the alternatives.

## Anti-Patterns

**Aspiring to glory instead of work.** Status, titles, and recognition are not useful. They're byproducts of useful work — and only when the work was actually useful. Don't optimize for them directly.

**Confusing activity with usefulness.** Busy is not useful. Many highly active people produce little actual value. The question is not "am I working hard?" but "is this work producing something that benefits others?"

**Positive-sum blindness.** Assuming that someone else's gain must come at your expense kills the motivation to create. Study how wealth is actually created — by building things that didn't exist, not by capturing things that did.

**Helping people who aren't asking.** Paternalistic "usefulness" — doing things for people that they didn't want — isn't useful. It's ego. Genuine usefulness requires understanding what people actually need, not what you assume they need.

## Quick Checklist

Before committing to any project or direction:
- [ ] Who specifically benefits from this? Can I name them?
- [ ] How much does their situation improve? By what factor?
- [ ] How many people does this reach?
- [ ] Total Utility = Scale × Depth — what's the rough number?
- [ ] What's the counterfactual if this doesn't happen?
- [ ] Am I creating value or redistributing it?
- [ ] Is this the most useful thing I could be doing with my time?
- [ ] A useful life is worth having lived — does this contribute to that?
