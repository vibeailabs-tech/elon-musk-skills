# Ultra Hardcore Work

"A maniacal sense of urgency is our operating principle."

The difference between Elon's companies and everyone else isn't just vision — it's operating velocity. SpaceX built the world's largest AI training cluster (Colossus, 100,000 GPUs) in 122 days. Ninety-two days later, it was doubled. Suppliers said 18–24 months. They did it in 4.

This isn't about working people into the ground. It's about eliminating the vast quantities of wasted motion, dumb requirements, premature optimization, and organizational friction that slow most companies down by 10x.

---

## The Algorithm

Elon mandates this five-step process across all his companies. The order is not optional — violating it causes cascading waste. He has personally made every mistake of going backwards on these steps.

**Step 1: Make your requirements less dumb.**
Every requirement came from a person. That person might be wrong. Requirements from smart people are the *most* dangerous because they're least likely to be questioned. Always ask: Who made this requirement? Why? Is that person still right? Requirements must be owned by a named person — not a department. If you can't name the human responsible, the requirement is suspect.

**Step 2: Try very hard to delete the part or process.**
If you're not adding back 10% of deleted things, you're not deleting enough. The bias toward adding "just in case" is overwhelming and wrong. Deliberately overcorrect — delete more than seems safe, expect to add some back. The cost of over-deletion is recoverable. The cost of accumulated complexity is not.

**Step 3: Simplify or optimize.**
Only after deleting everything you can. The most common smart-person mistake is optimizing something that should not exist. Don't do it. Simplify ruthlessly once the deletions are done.

**Step 4: Accelerate.**
Speed up only after the process is correct and simplified. Do not dig faster if you're digging the wrong hole. Accelerating a bad process wastes more resources faster.

**Step 5: Automate.**
Last. Never automate something you haven't fully simplified and validated. Premature automation locks in bad decisions at scale. Tesla tore hundreds of robots out of the factory floor because automation was applied too early.

## Maniacal Urgency

Time is the only true currency. It cannot be recovered.

**Speed is offense and defense simultaneously.** The SR-71 Blackbird was never shot down — its only defense was acceleration. Over 3,000 missiles fired at it. None hit. Speed beats armor.

Apply this to companies: a factory running at 2x speed is equivalent to two factories. Innovation speed is better IP protection than patents — if you're shipping what others copied 2 years ago, they're not catching up.

**Every unit of time has a dollar value.** When Tesla was doing $2B/week in revenue, a 30-minute meeting that changed a decision was worth $100M. Know the dollar value of an hour in your context. This reframes urgency from abstract pressure to concrete math.

**Parallelize everything.** Most teams serialize what can run in parallel. Every gestating element — legal, engineering, procurement, regulatory — should be in flight simultaneously. Serialized timelines are almost always wrong by default.

**Set aggressive timelines.** There is a "law of gaseous expansion" for schedules: work expands to fill the time given. Aggressive timelines are not fake — they're what Elon actually believes is achievable. They also force teams to question assumptions rather than pad estimates.

## Anti-Patterns

**Large or frequent meetings:** Large meetings dilute accountability and slow everything down. If a meeting isn't adding value to everyone in the room, most people shouldn't be there. Walk out if you're not contributing — it's not rude to leave; it's rude to make someone stay and waste their time.

**Chain-of-command communication:** If getting something done between departments requires 6 levels of approval, the product will reflect that friction. Information should flow directly to whoever needs it. Org-chart paths are for accountability, not communication.

**Automating before deleting:** The Tesla production line had to have a hole cut in the factory wall to remove hundreds of prematurely automated robots. Always validate and simplify before automating.

**Optimizing the wrong thing:** Spending cycles accelerating a process that should be deleted is negative work. Before improving anything, ask: should this exist?

**Padding timelines:** Schedules that leave room to be comfortable guarantee comfort, not results. If you're not occasionally late on ambitious timelines, your timelines aren't ambitious enough.

## Applying This

When the user has a process, project, or challenge, run it through The Algorithm:

1. **Surface the requirements** — Who made them? Why? Are they still valid?
2. **Deletion audit** — List every step, part, meeting, or handoff. What can be cut entirely?
3. **Simplification pass** — After deletion, what can be simplified?
4. **Speed assessment** — What's the current cycle time? What's blocking it? What can run in parallel?
5. **Automation question** — Only after the above: what should be automated?

Then calculate time cost: what is one day of delay worth in this context? Make urgency concrete, not motivational.

## Quick Checklist

Before accepting any process, timeline, or structure:
- [ ] Who owns each requirement? Can I name the person?
- [ ] What can be completely deleted from this process?
- [ ] Am I optimizing something that should not exist?
- [ ] What can run in parallel that is currently serialized?
- [ ] What is one day of delay actually costing?
- [ ] Is this timeline the most aggressive we genuinely believe is possible?
- [ ] Am I automating before I've finished deleting and simplifying?
