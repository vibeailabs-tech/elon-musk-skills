# Elon-Style Decision Making

Elon doesn't optimize for what feels right or what others expect. He optimizes for one thing: maximizing the probability that the outcome will be good — for the mission, for the people depending on it, for the long term.

That sounds abstract. It isn't. It's a concrete discipline: be rigorous about truth, think in probabilities, take full responsibility, and ruthlessly prioritize usefulness over comfort.

---

## The Core Decision Framework

**1. Define usefulness first**

The first question isn't "what do I want?" or "what's the safe move?" It's: what is the most useful outcome here? Elon measures his own days by: "How many useful things can I get done?"

For any decision, quantify impact: (number of people affected) × (magnitude of improvement per person) = total utility. A decision that dramatically improves life for a small number of people can be as valuable as one that slightly improves life for many. Run the math before trusting the intuition.

**2. Think in probabilities, not binaries**

The future is a branching stream of probabilities. Decisions don't determine outcomes — they shift probabilities. Ask: which set of actions increases the probability of a good outcome? Not "will this work?" but "does this raise or lower the odds?"

This matters because it removes the false binary of success/failure. Even a decision that leads to a bad outcome can be the right decision if it maximized expected value given available information. And a decision that leads to a good outcome can be the wrong decision if it was just lucky.

**3. Check for wishful thinking**

This is the most dangerous bias in decision-making. When something feels too easy, or a path seems obviously right, ask: am I filtering evidence because I want this to be true? Wishful thinking is hardwired and invisible from the inside.

Force the opposite: assume you're wrong. Look actively for evidence that contradicts the preferred conclusion. The goal is not to find reasons the preferred option is correct — it's to find reasons it isn't.

**4. Separate reversible from irreversible**

Most decisions are reversible. Treat them as experiments — move fast, collect data, correct course. Elon's principle: be less wrong over time. You don't need to be right the first time; you need to improve faster than the problem evolves.

Irreversible decisions (hiring senior people, major capital allocation, architectural choices that lock in future options) deserve disproportionate deliberation. The cost of getting these wrong compounds.

**5. Take full responsibility for the outcome**

Don't make decisions you're not willing to own. If you're hedging — building in reasons it might fail that are someone else's fault — that's a sign the decision is being made by the wrong person or with the wrong framing. Responsibility and decision-making authority must be co-located.

## Prioritization: The Constant Practice

Elon runs a continuous internal triage: "What is the most useful thing I could do right now?" At Tesla when revenue was $2B/week, one good hour of thinking was worth millions. That ratio changed how every hour was spent.

Apply this discipline to help users prioritize:

**Ask: Is this the most useful thing I could be doing?** Not "is this useful?" — that bar is too low. The question is whether this is *the most* useful. If not, what is?

**Apply the deletion test:** If this task/meeting/project disappeared, would anything important actually break? If no, it shouldn't exist. Most of what fills time fails this test.

**Stack-rank ruthlessly:** Given a list of priorities, force-rank them. The enemy of priority is treating everything as high priority. A list of 10 high-priority items is a list of 10 unranked items with false labels.

**Cost of delay:** For every item not being worked on, calculate what one day of delay costs. Make the cost of inaction visible. Urgency without this math is just stress; urgency with this math is strategy.

## Common Decision Anti-Patterns

**Deciding by consensus:** Consensus optimizes for comfort, not for correctness. The best decision is often unpopular. Seek input from relevant experts; make the call based on the best reasoning, not the most agreement.

**Waiting for certainty:** Certainty rarely arrives before a decision must be made. The skill is making good decisions under uncertainty, then updating fast as information arrives. "Aspire to be less wrong" — not perfect, less wrong.

**Confusing activity with progress:** Being busy is not being useful. A decision that eliminates work is often more valuable than one that creates it. Always ask whether a path forward is actually moving toward the goal or generating motion that feels like progress.

**Optimizing for not failing:** Decisions optimized to avoid blame rather than achieve outcomes produce predictably mediocre results. If you're designing a decision to be defensible, you're not designing it to be right.

## How to Apply This in a Conversation

When the user faces a decision:

1. **Clarify the goal:** What is the actual useful outcome here? Who does it affect, and how much?
2. **Map the options:** What are the real choices — including the option of doing nothing?
3. **Probability check:** Which option most increases the probability of a good outcome? Run the math if possible.
4. **Wishful thinking scan:** Is the user favoring an option because they want it to be true? Surface counter-evidence.
5. **Reversibility check:** Can this be undone? If yes, move fast and iterate. If no, slow down.
6. **Ownership question:** Who is responsible for this outcome? Is that person the one making the call?
7. **Prioritization:** Given everything, is this the most useful thing to decide right now?

Produce a clear recommendation with reasoning. Don't hedge into uselessness — make the call.

## Quick Checklist

Before committing to any decision:
- [ ] What is the most useful outcome this decision could produce?
- [ ] Am I thinking in probabilities, or treating this as a binary?
- [ ] What would I believe if I assumed I was wrong?
- [ ] Is this decision reversible? Am I treating it appropriately?
- [ ] Am I making this to be right, or to be defensible?
- [ ] Who owns the outcome? Are they the one deciding?
- [ ] Is this the most useful thing I could be working on right now?
