# Sequenced Strategy

"Build a sports car. Use that money to build an affordable car. Use that money to build an even more affordable car."

Tesla's master plan was three sentences. It guided a decade of product decisions. The logic is simple but most companies miss it: new technology is always expensive at low volume. So start where someone will pay a high price for your expensive thing, then use those margins to fund the volume that makes it cheap.

This skill applies that framework to any product or business.

---

## The Core Logic

When you're introducing genuinely new technology, you face two simultaneous problems:
1. **New technology problem** — bugs, unknowns, things that need iteration
2. **Low volume problem** — high cost per unit because you haven't achieved scale

Both problems make your product expensive. The question is: who will pay a high price for an expensive version of your thing?

The answer is almost always: a niche that values the specific benefits enough to overlook cost. For Tesla, that was performance car buyers who wanted the fastest 0-60 mph on the market and didn't care that it was electric. For SpaceX, it was commercial satellite operators who valued reliability and wanted an alternative to established providers.

**Enter at high price, low volume:**
- Make your mistakes on small scale — cheaper to fix
- Learn the real production challenges before committing to mass volume
- Generate margins that fund the next phase
- Build capabilities and reputation that carry forward

**Use the proceeds to fund mass market:**
- Each generation is cheaper because of scale and learning
- You're not asking investors to fund the entire journey from day one
- The premium product validates that the core technology works

## Tesla's Actual Sequence

**Phase 1 — Tesla Roadster ($100K+, ~2,500 units)**
Proved the technology worked. Built the engineering team. Generated enough margin and credibility to raise the next round. Made the point that electric cars could be desirable, not just practical.

**Phase 2 — Model S ($70-90K, tens of thousands of units)**
First real volume. Started climbing the learning curve on manufacturing. Demonstrated the technology at a larger scale. Generated the cash and credibility for Phase 3.

**Phase 3 — Model 3 ($35-50K, hundreds of thousands of units)**
The mass market car that was always the actual goal. Only possible because Phases 1 and 2 funded the manufacturing investment and proved the technology.

The lesson: "You want to make your mistakes at small scale, work the bugs out of the system, then reach for scale." Trying to go directly to mass market with unproven technology is how you go bankrupt.

## Applying This Framework

When the user is designing a product strategy, work through:

**Step 1: Identify the niche that will pay premium**
Who values what your technology uniquely offers, enough to pay above-market prices? This is your beachhead. It must be real — not "everyone will eventually want this" but "these specific people want this now and will pay."

**Step 2: Size the beachhead**
Is it big enough to generate real margins and real learning? Too small and you can't fund Phase 2. Too big and you're not actually niche — you're just trying to do everything at once.

**Step 3: Design Phase 1 for learning, not scale**
Make your first product at a scale where mistakes are recoverable. Low volume means high cost per unit — accept that. The value of Phase 1 is what you learn, not what you sell.

**Step 4: Define the bridge**
What specifically does Phase 1 generate — capital, learning, manufacturing capability, brand credibility — that makes Phase 2 possible? The sequence only works if each phase genuinely funds and enables the next.

**Step 5: Map the full ladder**
Where does this end? What is the mass-market version? What price point, what volume, what cost structure? Work backwards from that to understand what you're building toward.

## Anti-Patterns

**Trying to be everything immediately.** The pressure to address the whole market from day one leads to mediocre products for everyone instead of excellent products for someone. Start narrow and excellent.

**Premium positioning without genuine differentiation.** The premium price requires genuine superiority on the dimensions your niche cares about. Tesla Roadster was actually the fastest accelerating production car on the market. If your premium product is just expensive, not genuinely better, the sequence fails at Step 1.

**Using Phase 1 margins for the wrong things.** The point of premium margins is to fund the transition to mass market — better manufacturing, lower cost structures, more volume. Using them for overhead and headcount instead of capability building breaks the sequence.

**Skipping phases under pressure.** Investors and press will push for faster progress to the mass market. The pressure to skip Phase 2 and go directly from niche to mass is how you break the system. Each phase builds what the next phase requires.

---

## Quick Checklist

When designing a sequenced product strategy:
- [ ] Who is my beachhead — the specific niche that will pay premium for my technology now?
- [ ] What genuine advantage do I offer that justifies the premium price?
- [ ] Is the beachhead large enough to generate real learning and real margins?
- [ ] What do I learn in Phase 1 that makes Phase 2 possible?
- [ ] What does Phase 1 generate (capital, capability, credibility) that funds Phase 2?
- [ ] What is the full sequence — what is the eventual mass-market product?
- [ ] Am I making mistakes at small scale before committing to mass production?
