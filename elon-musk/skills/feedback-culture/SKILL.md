# Feedback Culture

"All bad news should be given loudly and often. Good news can be said quietly and once."

This one rule inverts everything about how most teams operate. Most teams celebrate wins loudly and bury problems quietly. The result: problems compound in silence while the team celebrates surface metrics. Elon's rule forces the inversion — surface problems immediately, to everyone who needs to know, at full volume.

This skill builds the conditions where that culture can exist.

---

## Why Most Feedback Cultures Fail

The default is comfort-seeking. Managers want to be liked. Teams want harmony. This produces:
- Problems that could be caught early going unreported for weeks
- Mediocre performance being tolerated because confronting it is uncomfortable
- Leaders making decisions based on optimistic summaries instead of ground truth
- "Camaraderie" that suppresses honest assessment

"Wanting to be everyone's friend leads you to care too much about the emotions of the individual in front of you rather than caring about the success of the whole enterprise. Focusing on that one individual can lead to a far greater number of people being hurt."

The math: protecting one person from hard feedback costs everyone on the team who depends on that person performing. The compassionate-seeming act is actually the cruel one.

---

## The Core Rules

**Bad news loudly and often. Good news quietly and once.**
Bad news compounds when hidden. A problem discovered at week 1 costs 10x less to fix than the same problem discovered at week 8. The team needs to know about problems when they're small. Create explicit norms that surface problems immediately — reward the reporting, not just the fixing.

**Criticize the action, not the person.**
"I give people hardcore feedback, but I try to always focus on the substance of the discussion. I try to criticize the action, not the person. We all make mistakes. What matters is whether a person has a good feedback loop, can seek criticism from others, and can improve."

The distinction: "This code has a design flaw that will cause scaling problems" vs "You don't think carefully about scaling." One is actionable and about the work. One is a character judgment. The first produces improvement; the second produces defensiveness.

**Physics doesn't care about hurt feelings.**
"Physics does not care about hurt feelings. It cares about whether you got the rocket right." Reality delivers hard feedback without softening it. The team that gets hard feedback from each other internally is less surprised and better prepared when reality delivers it externally.

**Don't want to be liked.**
"I think it's a real weakness to want to be liked. A real weakness." This is not permission to be unkind. It's permission to be honest even when honesty is uncomfortable. The two are not the same thing.

## Building the Culture

**Model it from the top.**
Feedback culture requires the leader to receive feedback publicly and respond non-defensively. If leaders punish people who bring bad news, the bad news stops coming. If they reward it — even when it's uncomfortable — the flow of truth continues.

**Reward problem-surfacing.**
When someone surfaces a problem early, treat that as a success, not a failure. The failure was the problem existing; the success was catching it early. These need different emotional responses.

**Kill the camaraderie trap.**
Close-knit teams develop norms against criticizing each other's work. "There is a tendency to not want to throw a colleague under the bus." This is loyalty mislabeled as virtue. Genuine loyalty to a colleague means telling them when their work isn't good enough — before the market, the product, or a crisis does.

**Make feedback specific and immediate.**
Vague feedback ("this isn't quite right") is not useful. Specific feedback ("this component fails the idiot index test — the raw material costs $50 and we're paying $800") is actionable. Immediate feedback closes the loop while the work is fresh and correctable.

**Separate feedback from performance reviews.**
Quarterly reviews create artificial delays in the feedback cycle. Problems that arise in January should be addressed in January, not during a review in March. Build continuous feedback loops — short, frequent, specific.

## Assessing Whether Someone Has a Good Feedback Loop

Elon's standard: "What matters is whether a person has a good feedback loop, can seek criticism from others, and can improve."

When evaluating someone on a team, the key questions are:
- Do they actively seek feedback, or wait for it to be imposed?
- Do they incorporate feedback and improve, or defend and rationalize?
- Do they surface problems they see, or wait for others to find them?
- When they're wrong, do they say so — or explain why they were actually right?

People who can seek criticism and improve are high-leverage team members regardless of their starting level. People who can't, regardless of their talent, create information bottlenecks and culture drag.

---

## Quick Checklist

For any team or feedback interaction:
- [ ] Is bad news surfacing immediately and loudly?
- [ ] Is good news proportionally quieter?
- [ ] Is feedback on the work, not the character?
- [ ] Does leadership receive and respond to feedback non-defensively?
- [ ] Are people rewarded for surfacing problems early?
- [ ] Is camaraderie suppressing honest assessment?
- [ ] Does every person on the team actively seek criticism?
- [ ] Is the feedback loop tight and continuous — or delayed and periodic?
