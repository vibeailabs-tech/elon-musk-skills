# Physics Thinking

"Physics is law. Everything else is a recommendation."

Elon's mental operating system is built on one obsession: truth. Not what he wants to be true, not what others believe, not what feels comfortable — what is actually, verifiably true. This comes directly from physics, where reality is the final judge and no amount of wishful thinking changes the outcome.

This skill applies that mindset to any problem. It's a rigorous truth-seeking process — not to be negative, but because correct beliefs are the only foundation for good decisions.

---

## The Core Discipline: Obsess Over Truth

Physics attempts to understand the truth of the universe. It finds truths that have predictive power — things you can test, verify, and build on. Elon extended this to all domains: business, engineering, strategy, even personal decisions.

The practice has three components:

**1. Start from axioms, not analogies**
What do you know is true at the most fundamental level? Not "what have others done?" or "what does conventional wisdom say?" — what can you verify from the ground up?

**2. Reason forward, check against reality**
Build your conclusions from first principles. Then test them against observable facts. If your conclusion conflicts with reality, your axioms or your reasoning are wrong — find which.

**3. Aspire to be less wrong**
The goal is not to be right. The goal is to be less wrong over time. "It's OK to be wrong. Just don't be confident and wrong." This means: hold beliefs proportional to the evidence, not inversely proportional to it. Update when new evidence arrives.

---

## Eliminating Wishful Thinking

Wishful thinking is the most dangerous bias in any decision-maker's toolkit. It operates invisibly: you want something to be true, so you unconsciously filter evidence that contradicts it and amplify evidence that supports it.

The cure is not pessimism. It's discipline.

**When evaluating any belief or plan, ask:**
- Am I believing this because evidence supports it, or because I want it to be true?
- What would I believe if I had the opposite emotional stake in the outcome?
- What evidence would change my mind? If I can't name it, I'm not reasoning — I'm rationalizing.
- Does this feel too easy? Too convenient? That's a signal of wishful thinking.

Elon's rule: "That's why I always assume we're losing, even when it looks like we might win." This is not pessimism — it's a deliberate correction for the natural human bias toward optimism.

**Pressure-test any idea by asking:**
- What has to be true for this to work?
- What's the probability each of those things is actually true?
- If all of them must be true simultaneously, what are the combined odds?

## The Physics Checklist for Any Belief or Plan

Run this before committing to any major conclusion:

**Am I violating physics?**
Not metaphorically — literally. Does this plan require something that breaks conservation of energy, thermodynamics, or basic math? If yes, it doesn't work. If no, it might.

**Do I have the right axioms?**
What are the foundational assumptions this conclusion rests on? Are those assumptions actually true, or are they inherited from convention? Name them explicitly.

**Am I making valid inferences?**
Given those axioms, does the conclusion actually follow? Many reasoning errors are logical, not factual — the facts are right but the inference is wrong.

**What does reality say?**
What observable evidence bears on this conclusion? Is that evidence consistent with the conclusion or in tension with it? Evidence in tension is more important than evidence in support.

**How wrong could I be?**
Assume you're wrong. What's the most likely way? What would that failure look like? If you can't imagine being wrong, you're not thinking rigorously — you're defending a position.

## Truth as a Competitive Advantage

Most people and organizations don't operate this way. They operate on inherited beliefs, social consensus, and motivated reasoning. This creates systematic errors that persist for years or decades — and enormous opportunity for anyone reasoning from actual truth.

"Do you have the right fundamental axioms, or truths? Are they relevant? Are you making the right conclusions based on those truths? That's the essence of critical thinking, and yet it is amazing how often people fail to do that."

When helping the user think through any problem, surface their hidden assumptions. Make them explicit. Check them against reality. This is the work that produces non-obvious insights.

---

## The Knowledge Tree

Treat all knowledge as a semantic tree. Understand the trunk and big branches before getting into the leaves — otherwise the details have nothing to hang on to. Surface-level knowledge without foundational understanding produces confident ignorance, the most dangerous kind.

The physicist's approach to learning: get the fundamentals right, then build outward. Master the underlying structure before the specific applications. A solid foundation in how something actually works outperforms memorized facts every time.

## Quick Checklist

Before accepting any conclusion as valid:
- [ ] What are the foundational axioms this rests on? Are they actually true?
- [ ] Am I inferring correctly from those axioms?
- [ ] Am I believing this because I want it to be true?
- [ ] What evidence would change my mind? Can I name it?
- [ ] Does this violate any hard constraints (physics, math, logic)?
- [ ] Have I assumed I'm wrong and looked for the most likely failure mode?
- [ ] Am I holding this belief proportional to the evidence for it?
- [ ] What does reality — not convention, not consensus — actually say?
