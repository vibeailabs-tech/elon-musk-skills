# Knowledge Tree

"It is important to view knowledge as a semantic tree. Make sure you understand the fundamental principles — the trunk and big branches — before you get into the leaves. Otherwise there is nothing for the details to hang on to."

Elon became expert in aerospace engineering, automotive manufacturing, energy storage, tunneling, and AI — not through formal education in each, but through a deliberate approach to learning. He reads voraciously, seeks fundamentals first, and builds mental models that transfer across domains.

This skill teaches that approach.

---

## The Core Framework

Knowledge has structure. It's not a flat list of facts — it's a tree with roots, trunk, branches, and leaves.

**Roots:** The underlying math, physics, or logic that governs a domain. For rockets, that's orbital mechanics and thermodynamics. For business, that's supply and demand, incentives, and accounting identity. For programming, that's computation theory and data structures.

**Trunk:** The core principles that dominate the domain. The 20% of knowledge that explains 80% of what happens. The things every expert in the field agrees on.

**Branches:** The major sub-domains, specialties, and application areas. Where the field divides.

**Leaves:** Specific facts, techniques, tools, conventions. The details.

**The error:** Starting with leaves. Memorizing facts without understanding the principles behind them produces brittle knowledge — you can answer familiar questions but can't reason about new ones.

**The right approach:** Get the trunk solid first. Then branches. Then leaves. Details attach to structure; without structure, they fall off.

## How Elon Learns

**Read at maximum data rate.** Speech transfers information at a few hundred bits per second. Reading is several times faster. Elon skipped many college lectures for this reason — the data rate was too slow. Optimize for learning speed.

**Read broadly, then deeply.** Start with a broad survey of the entire knowledge landscape. You can't know what you're most interested in, or what will be most useful, without first knowing what exists. Elon read the Encyclopedia Britannica as a child — not comprehensively, but broadly enough to build a map of the domain space.

**Talk to experts.** Reading gives you foundational structure. Experts give you pattern recognition — what actually matters, what the field gets wrong, what the cutting-edge questions are. Seek out the best people in any field you're entering and ask them what they'd want to know if they were starting again.

**Cross-pollinate.** "What is simple in one arena is often profound in another." The most valuable knowledge transfers are from domains where something is understood deeply to domains where it isn't yet. Elon applies physics thinking to manufacturing. He applies software iteration speed to rocket development. These cross-domain transfers produce non-obvious breakthroughs.

**Teach yourself.** In rocketry, Elon is self-taught. He bought textbooks and read them. He called experts. He asked questions that "dumb" people ask because he didn't know the conventions that would have constrained a trained engineer. Sometimes not knowing what "can't" be done is an advantage.

## Building a Learning Plan

When the user wants to learn a new domain, structure the plan as follows:

**Step 1: Map the tree**
Ask: What are the foundational disciplines that govern this field? What does every expert agree on? What are the major sub-domains? Get a rough map before diving in.

**Step 2: Get the trunk**
Find the 2-3 books or resources that cover the core principles most thoroughly. Read these first, even if they're hard. Struggle with the fundamentals — it makes everything else easier.

**Step 3: Find the best experts**
Who are the 3-5 people in the world most respected in this field? What do they write? Who do they cite? Read them directly.

**Step 4: Read broadly at the leaves**
Once the trunk is solid, read widely in adjacent areas. Look for cross-domain transfers. What does this field borrow from others? What does it offer others?

**Step 5: Apply immediately**
Knowledge that isn't applied decays. Find a project, problem, or experiment that requires using what you've learned. The application reveals gaps the reading didn't.

**Step 6: Seek feedback from reality**
Build something. Test something. Get your conclusions checked against what actually happens. Reality is the final teacher.

## Anti-Patterns

**Starting with tools instead of fundamentals.** Learning a framework before understanding what it's built on. Learning a programming language before understanding computation. You'll be fast at familiar tasks and helpless at novel ones.

**Passive reading without synthesis.** Reading without stopping to ask "what does this mean?" and "how does this connect to what I already know?" produces the illusion of learning without the substance.

**Optimizing for credentials over understanding.** Degrees and certifications measure exposure to material, not understanding of it. The self-taught expert who built something real often knows more than the credentialed one who never applied anything.

**Stopping at what's comfortable.** The most important knowledge is usually past the point of initial difficulty. Push through the hard parts — that's where the real understanding lives.

## Quick Checklist

When starting to learn any new domain:
- [ ] What are the roots — the underlying math, physics, or logic?
- [ ] What's the trunk — the 20% of principles that explain 80% of outcomes?
- [ ] What are the 2-3 foundational books every expert in this field has read?
- [ ] Who are the best experts to talk to or read directly?
- [ ] What cross-domain transfers might apply here?
- [ ] What can I build or test immediately to apply this?
- [ ] Am I learning the tree from trunk to leaves, or jumping straight to leaves?
