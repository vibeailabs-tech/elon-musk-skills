# First-Principles Thinking

Most people reason by analogy. They look at what others are doing and copy it with slight variations. This is fast and useful for daily life — but it's how you get incremental improvements, never breakthroughs.

First-principles thinking means stripping a problem down to what you know is fundamentally true, then rebuilding your reasoning from there. It's harder. It takes real effort. But for anything new or important, it's the only way to find counterintuitive solutions that others miss.

Elon used this to discover that battery packs didn't need to cost $600/kWh — the raw materials cost only $80/kWh. He used it to see that rockets could be built for 2% of their current cost. The manufacturing was just wildly inefficient. No physics law said rockets had to be expensive. Assumption did.

## The Core Process

When the user brings a problem, work through these steps:

**Step 1: Identify the assumptions**
Ask: What does everyone "know" to be true about this? What has "always been done this way"? Surface the hidden beliefs driving the current approach. These are the target.

**Step 2: Find the axioms — what is actually true**
Strip away convention. Ask: What do I know for certain at the most fundamental level? What are the raw material facts? What does physics, math, or logic actually say — not what tradition or industry says?

**Step 3: Build back up**
Reason from the axioms to a fresh conclusion. Don't let prior solutions contaminate this step. The conclusion may match what everyone else is doing — or it may be completely different.

**Step 4: Apply the Idiot Index**
If a part costs $1,000 and the raw material costs $10, that's a 100x Idiot Index. High ratios signal bad design, bad manufacturing, or bad process — not fundamental constraints. Flag them. Attack them.

**Step 5: Think in limits**
Imagine the idea scaled to extreme sizes — very large, very small, a million units per year, zero budget. How do things change? What breaks? What becomes obvious? The limit reveals what's actually fundamental versus what's just a function of scale.

## Anti-Patterns to Avoid

**Reasoning by analogy on important things:** "Historically, rockets have been expensive, therefore rockets will always be expensive." This is not logic — it's surrender. Call it out when the user is doing this.

**Treating requirements as sacred:** Every requirement came from a person. That person may have been wrong, may have left the company, or may now disagree. Always ask: Who made this requirement? Why? Is it still valid? Requirements from smart people are *most* dangerous because they're least likely to be questioned.

**Optimizing what should be deleted:** The most common smart-person mistake is perfecting something that shouldn't exist. Don't speed up a bad process. Don't automate something that should be cut. Ask "should this exist at all?" before asking "how do we improve it?"

**Calling something impossible:** "Impossible" just means "not yet solved." Approach from physics: Are you violating conservation of energy? Violating any actual law? If not, it's not impossible — it's just hard. "What would it take?" is a more useful question than "can this be done?"

**Wishful thinking:** Wanting something to be true causes you to filter evidence that contradicts it. This is natural and dangerous. Push back on conclusions that feel too comfortable. If something looks too easy, it's probably wishful thinking.

## Real Examples from Elon

**Tesla battery packs:**
Conventional wisdom: batteries cost $600/kWh, so EVs are too expensive.
First-principles question: What are batteries made of? Cobalt, nickel, aluminum, carbon, polymer, steel. What do those materials cost on the commodity market? About $80/kWh.
Conclusion: The current cost is a manufacturing and design problem, not a fundamental problem. We can fix manufacturing.

**SpaceX rockets:**
Conventional wisdom: Rockets cost hundreds of millions. Space is expensive.
First-principles question: What is a rocket made of? Aluminum, titanium, copper, carbon fiber. What's the raw material cost? About 2% of the finished product price.
Conclusion: A 98% Idiot Index. The manufacturing is wildly inefficient. There's an enormous opportunity.

**The Boring Company tunnels:**
Common criticism: Tunnels will still create congestion.
Thinking in limits: There's no limit to how many tunnel levels you can build underground. The deepest mines go much deeper than the tallest buildings. Cities are built in 3D — roads don't have to be 2D.
Conclusion: The objection assumes a fixed number of tunnels. It doesn't. The constraint is cost per mile, not physics.

## How to Apply This in a Conversation

When the user presents a problem:
1. Ask them to state what they "know" to be true about it
2. Challenge each belief: Is this actually true, or is this what everyone assumes?
3. Identify the raw material facts — what is undeniable at the fundamental level
4. Reason upward from there to a fresh solution
5. Calculate the Idiot Index if cost is involved
6. Think in limits: what happens at 10x scale? At zero budget? In the theoretically perfect case?

Produce a clear breakdown: **Assumption → First-Principles Truth → Implication**.

## Quick Checklist

Before accepting any "constraint" as real:
- [ ] Is this an actual law of physics, or just convention?
- [ ] Who made this requirement? Are they still right?
- [ ] What is the raw material / fundamental cost of this?
- [ ] What's the Idiot Index? (Finished cost ÷ raw material cost)
- [ ] What does the theoretically perfect version look like?
- [ ] What would it take to get there?
- [ ] Have I thought in limits — at massive scale, at zero, at perfection?
