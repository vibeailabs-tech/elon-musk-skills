# Building Companies

"A company is just a bunch of people coming together to create a product or service."

That's it. No mysticism. The output of any company is the vector sum of the people within it — their direction and magnitude. If you have exceptional people aligned on a worthy goal, you get exceptional output. Everything else is detail.

Most companies fail this at two levels: they don't attract exceptional people, and they don't create conditions where exceptional people can produce exceptional work. Elon's obsession is solving both.

---

## What a Company Actually Is

A company is a cybernetic collective — people and machines together, smarter than any individual. It has one job: create products or services that are genuinely useful to people.

If the product is great, the company succeeds. Everything else — fundraising, PR, org charts, culture decks — is in service of that. Lose sight of the product and you lose the company.

The first thing to build is a prototype. Not a deck. Not a roadmap. A physical object that someone can touch. Anything can look good on PowerPoint. A working prototype, even primitive, does what a thousand slides cannot.

**The test of a company:** Do you have a compelling product at a compelling price? If no to either — you don't have a great company yet.

## Building Exceptional Teams

The fundamental constraint in building anything ambitious is not money. It is exceptional people. There are not that many. They are hard to find, harder to attract, and easy to lose.

**Recruit like it matters — because it does.** A small group of technically exceptional people will always beat a large group of moderately skilled people. The output quality gap is non-linear. Elon called a candidate's wife's employer (Google) to arrange a transfer so the candidate could join SpaceX. That's what recruiting hard looks like.

**Look for evidence of exceptional ability, not credentials.** Ask people to walk through the hardest problems they've solved. Ask for details. The person who actually solved the problem knows every detail — the pain, the wrong turns, the eventual fix. The person who just watched it happen does not. Dig into specifics.

**Hire for attitude, not just skill.** Skills can be taught. Attitude requires a brain transplant. Intellect without heart is a mistake Elon says he's made and corrected. Character is revealed by the character of someone's friends and associates — they don't perform the same mask.

**Attract through mission, not perks.** The best engineers in the world can work anywhere. They choose based on: Is the work meaningful? Will I learn? Will my talent actually be used? A compelling mission, used to its full potential, and real financial upside — that's the offer that wins.

**Set the bar at Special Forces.** The minimum passing grade is excellent. Mediocre performance must not be tolerated — not because it's cruel, but because it pulls down everyone around it and signals that standards don't matter. Elon: "Only exceptional performance constitutes a passing grade."

## Culture and Feedback

**Create an environment where great engineers can flourish.** This is a primary CEO responsibility. Not managing people — giving exceptional people a context where their capability gets fully deployed.

**Feedback over feelings.** Physics doesn't care about hurt feelings. Neither should your review culture. Give hard feedback focused on the work, not the person. Criticize the action, not the character. The goal is a tight feedback loop — people who can seek criticism, absorb it, and improve.

**Bad news loudly and often. Good news quietly and once.** This inverts the natural human tendency. Bad news compounds if hidden. Surface it immediately, to everyone who needs it.

**Don't manage through popularity.** Wanting to be liked by your team is a real weakness. It leads to protecting individuals at the expense of the whole. Camaraderie that suppresses honest feedback is organizational rot dressed up as culture.

## Organizational Design

**Kill the chain of command for communication.** Chain of command works for accountability. It kills communication speed. Information should flow directly to whoever needs it — not through six layers of managers. If a product has organizational friction, you'll see it in the product. Poor communication between departments shows up as seams and failures in what you build.

**Remove organizational boundaries.** Allow anyone to talk directly to anyone. If an engineer needs to communicate with another department, they should be able to do it directly. Requiring everything to flow upward and back down is friction that degrades both speed and quality.

**Simplify constantly.** Complexity accumulates. Every layer, every process, every approval step has to earn its place. Left unchecked, organizations become immune to their own inefficiency. The cure is deliberate, aggressive simplification — permanently.

---

## Company-Building Sequence

When helping someone build or fix a company, think through these in order:

1. **Product first:** Is there a compelling product at a compelling price? If not, everything else is premature.
2. **Who's on the team:** Is the team exceptional? Where are the gaps? What's the recruiting plan?
3. **Culture check:** Are standards actually enforced? Is feedback real? Is camaraderie suppressing honesty?
4. **Org design:** Where is communication getting blocked by structure? What can be cut from the approval chain?
5. **Mission clarity:** Is the purpose compelling enough to attract the best? Do people know what winning looks like?

## Quick Checklist

Before making any company decision:
- [ ] Does this serve the product? If not, why are we doing it?
- [ ] Who is the best person for this role — not who is available, but who is best?
- [ ] Is the feedback culture producing honest, fast improvement loops?
- [ ] Where is org structure slowing down communication that should be direct?
- [ ] Is our bar for performance actually high, or are we protecting mediocrity?
- [ ] Is the mission compelling enough to attract people who have other options?
